Extended Phonological Adaptation Rules

Companion to “Living Among Languages: A Framework for Code-Switching Text-to-Speech Synthesis Across Indian Languages.” Extends the rules in the main paper for the evaluated language pairs — Bengali–Odia, Hindi–Nepali, Hindi–Punjabi, and Tamil–Telugu — following the same three rule categories (inventory alignment, prosodic/schwa adaptation, cluster repair) and derivation methodology described in Section III-C.

Bengali–OdiaClosely related Eastern Indo-Aryan languages with largely overlapping phoneme inventories

R6Sibilant merger transfer

Bengali merges the historical three-way sibilant contrast (/s/, /ʃ/, /sʰ/) to a single postalveolar /ʃ/ in most positions, whereas Odia retains a plain dental /s/. Bengali speakers producing Odia words carry over this merger.

/s/ → /ʃ/

ExampleOdia “଎ুन্দर” /sundara/ (“beautiful”) → Bengali-accented /ʃundara/

R7Mid-vowel regularization (shared category with R2)

Odia distinguishes /ɔ/ and /o/ more consistently across contexts than Bengali, which neutralizes the contrast in casual speech. Bengali speakers regularize Odia's contextual /ɔ/ realizations toward Bengali's more stable /o/.

/ɔ/ → /o/  (where Bengali does not license /ɔ/)

ExampleOdia “ଆल” /bʰɔlɔ/ (“good”) → Bengali-accented /bʰolo/

Hindi–NepaliShare Devanagari orthography and most of the consonant inventory, differ in allophonic and prosodic detail

R8Retroflex flap neutralization

Nepali has an intervocalic retroflex flap /ɛ˞/ as an allophone distinct from the stop /ə˞/; Hindi does not maintain this contrast as robustly outside a small set of loanwords. Hindi speakers realize Nepali /ɛ˞/ as /ə˞/.

/ɛ˞/ → /ə˞/  (intervocalic)

ExampleNepali “पढ्ने” /paɛ˞ʰne/ (“to read”) → Hindi-accented /paə˞ʰne/

R9Medial vowel-length shortening

Nepali maintains phonemic long/short vowel contrasts in medial syllables more strictly than Hindi, which tends to shorten unstressed medial long vowels. Hindi speakers shorten Nepali medial long vowels outside the stressed syllable.

Vː → V  /  (unstressed, medial)

ExampleNepali “बिहान” /bihaːna/ (“morning”) → Hindi-accented /bihana/

Hindi–PunjabiPunjabi lost Hindi's voiced aspirated stops to lexical tone via historical tonogenesis

R10Tone-to-aspiration substitution

Where Punjabi has a plain consonant plus a lexical tone (historically derived from a voiced aspirate), Hindi speakers — who do not natively produce Punjabi's tone contrasts — substitute the historical voiced aspirated consonant instead of realizing the tone.

C + Tone(low) → Cʰ  (voiced aspirated, tone dropped)

ExamplePunjabi “୦ୡ୩ୣ” (tonal, historically from /ɡʰoːɛ˞aː/, “horse”) → Hindi-accented /ɡʰoːɛ˞aː/

R11Nasalization under-realization

Punjabi has extensive vowel nasalization arising from historical nasal-consonant loss with compensatory nasalization on the preceding vowel. Hindi speakers tend to under-nasalize and instead retain a weak nasal consonant.

Ṽ → VN  (nasal consonant retained, nasalization reduced)

ExamplePunjabi “୛୥୼” /kuɛ̃iː/ (“girl”) → Hindi-accented /kuɛniː/-like, lighter nasalized vowel with a perceptible nasal onset

Tamil–TeluguTamil's native phonology lacks phonemic voicing/aspiration; Telugu has a full stop series via Sanskrit borrowing

R12Voicing/aspiration neutralization

Telugu contrasts /k, kʰ, ɡ, ɡʰ/ (largely via Sanskrit loanwords) that Tamil's native inventory does not distinguish. Tamil speakers collapse these to a single unaspirated stop, with voicing determined allophonically rather than phonemically.

{k, kʰ, ɡ, ɡʰ} → k  (elsewhere); → ɡ  / V__V (intervocalic)

ExampleTelugu “డర్చు” /kʰartʃu/ (“expense”) → Tamil-accented /kartʃu/

R13Cluster repair via prothesis

Tamil disallows word-initial /s/+consonant clusters found in Sanskrit-derived Telugu vocabulary and English loanwords common in code-switched speech. Tamil speakers repair these with a prothetic vowel rather than the word-medial epenthesis seen in R5.

#sC → #isC  (word-initial prothesis)

ExampleTelugu/English loanword “స్కస్” / “school” /skuːl/ → Tamil-accented /iskuːl/
